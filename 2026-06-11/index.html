Google DeepMind Unveils Gemini 3.0 with Episodic Memory

DeepMind released Gemini 3.0, a large multimodal model integrating a persistent episodic memory module that retains context across sessions. The architecture combines a sparse MoE with a dedicated memory controller that writes to an external knowledge store, enabling efficient long-term recall without catastrophic forgetting. A novel "reflection loop" allows autonomous self-correction of reasoning errors. Gemini 3.0 achieves 96.2% on MMLU-PRO and state-of-the-art results on the MATH-OMNIBUS dataset. DeepMind open-sourced the memory controller API. Source

OpenAI Launches o4-mini: Efficient Reasoning with Sparse Attention

OpenAI released o4-mini, a compact reasoning model optimized for real-time multi-step reasoning. It uses sparse attention to dynamically prune irrelevant tokens during the reasoning chain, reducing inference latency ~40% versus o3-mini while matching its GPQA (86.4%) and AIME 2026 (71.3%) scores. A "branch-and-prune" decoding technique explores multiple reasoning paths in parallel and selects the most consistent one. A quantized INT8 version is available for edge deployment. Source

Microsoft Research: LoRA-X and DPO-2 for Multimodal Fine-Tuning

Microsoft published LoRA-X and DPO-2, extending parameter-efficient fine-tuning to large multimodal models. LoRA-X factorizes attention projections across vision, language, and audio encoders into a shared low-rank subspace, requiring ~10x fewer trainable parameters than standard LoRA. DPO-2 improves direct preference optimization with a "preference consistency" loss that penalizes contradictory pairwise preferences, yielding 5-8% relative improvement on instruction following and safety alignment. Code and adapters released under MIT. Source

Meta AI Releases Llama 4.1: 1-Bit Quantization and 2M Context

Meta launched Llama 4.1 (8B, 70B, 405B) with a novel 1-bit ternary quantization scheme (values \(-1, 0, +1\)) achieving accuracy within 1.5% of full-precision on MMLU and HellaSwag. The model introduces personalized on-device self-supervision for fine-tuning on user interactions without transmitting data to the cloud, plus a 2M-token context window via a sliding-window-of-sliding-windows attention mechanism. Source

Apple Foundation Language Model v2: On-Device Multimodal

Apple published a technical report on their second-generation 3.2B-parameter foundation model powering iOS 20 and macOS 16. Trained via a privacy-safe federated learning pipeline, it processes text, images, and audio with a new quantized attention mechanism compressing the KV cache to 2 bits per element. The full model runs on a single A18 Pro chip with ~6 GB memory footprint, surpassing GPT-4o-mini on the LMArena multimodal benchmark. Source

Redesign Mixture-of-Experts Routers with Manifold Power Iteration

Authors: Songhao Wu, Ang Lv, Ruobing Xie, Yankai Lin. Venue: arXiv preprint, June 2026.

The authors identify a fundamental flaw in MoE router design: router rows are trained as differentiable proxies for expert selection, but no principle enforces that the rows encode the expert matrices they gate. They propose aligning each router row with the principal singular direction of its corresponding expert matrix via Manifold Power Iteration (MPI). The "Power-then-Retract" paradigm runs a power iteration step on router weights, then retracts to enforce a norm constraint. Theoretically, MPI guarantees convergence of router rows toward principal singular directions. Empirically validated at 1B-11B parameter scales, demonstrating improved token-expert affinity and downstream performance. arXiv:2606.12397

APPO: Agentic Procedural Policy Optimization

Authors: Xucong Wang, Ziyu Ma, Yong Wang et al. Venue: arXiv preprint, June 2026.

Current agentic RL methods assign credit over coarse heuristics (tool-call boundaries, fixed workflows), missing the fine-grained decision points that actually determine outcomes. APPO introduces a Branching Score combining token uncertainty with policy-induced likelihood gains of subsequent continuations, enabling targeted exploration while filtering spurious high-entropy positions. Procedure-level advantage scaling distributes credit across branched rollouts more precisely. Across 13 benchmarks, APPO improves strong agentic RL baselines by nearly 4 points while maintaining efficient tool-call usage and interpretability. arXiv:2606.12384

On Subquadratic Architectures: From Applications to Principles

Authors: Hartl, Zólyomi, Stap, Hoedt, Schmidinger, Hauzenberger, Böck, Klambauer, Hochreiter. Venue: arXiv preprint, June 2026.

This paper provides the most comprehensive head-to-head comparison yet of three leading subquadratic architectures — xLSTM, Mamba-2, and Gated DeltaNet — across code-model pre-training, LLM distillation, and time-series foundation models. xLSTM delivers the strongest overall performance. Through a unified formulation analysis, the authors attribute the advantage to xLSTM's gating scheme enabling more flexible and stable memory correction. Controlled synthetic length-generalization tasks confirm that robust state tracking and accumulation are the key drivers. This is significant for anyone building sequence models beyond quadratic attention. arXiv:2606.12364

Fourier Features Let Agents Learn High Precision Policies with Imitation Learning

Authors: Balázs Gyenes, Emiliyan Gospodinov, Jan Frieling et al. Venue: ICML 2026.

RGB-only policies for robotic manipulation suffer from depth ambiguity and perspective scale issues. Point-cloud policies offer stronger geometric priors but performance remains task-dependent. The authors hypothesize spectral bias of neural networks toward low-frequency functions as the culprit. Their fix is elegantly simple: map point clouds from Cartesian space into high-dimensional Fourier space before feeding them into the encoder. Validated on RoboCasa, ManiSkill3, and a real robot setup, Fourier features provide significant benefits across diverse encoder architectures and are robust to hyperparameter choices. arXiv:2606.12334

Anatomy of Post-Training: Interpretability for Data Characterization

Authors: Leon Bergen, Usha Bhalla, Sidharth Baskaran et al. (17 authors). Venue: arXiv preprint, June 2026.

A large collaboration tackling the black-box nature of RLHF/DPO post-training. The authors introduce a data-centric pipeline using interpretability protocols to: (1) inspect preference datasets before optimization, (2) develop statistical hypotheses about latent concepts separating preferred from dispreferred generations, and (3) shape the reward signal via feature-level or data-level interventions. The framework unifies several interpretability-based training protocols under a common conceptual umbrella. Demonstrated on diagnosing spurious correlations, mitigating sycophancy, and amplifying desired model properties like safety guardrails. arXiv:2606.12360

Position: Hippocampal Explicit Memory Is the Cornerstone for AGI

Author: Sangjun Park. Venue: ICML 2026 (Position Paper Track).

This position paper draws a direct parallel between LLM limitations and human implicit memory, arguing that hippocampal explicit memory (declarative, episodic) is the missing ingredient for AGI. The author notes that LLMs' implicit statistical learning cannot produce higher-order cognitive functions like long-term strategic planning, metacognition, and symbolic reasoning — these require explicit memory binding, pattern separation, and context-dependent retrieval. The paper provides a computational blueprint for integrating hippocampal-style explicit memory into LLMs, bridging neuroscience and AI. arXiv:2606.11245

Bebop: Breaking Entropy Bounds in RL Training via MTP with Rejection Sampling

Authors: Yucheng Li, Huiqiang Jiang, Yang Xu et al. Venue: arXiv preprint, June 2026.

Multi-Token Prediction (MTP) for speculative decoding offers a natural way to accelerate RL rollouts, but acceptance rates degrade during training. Bebop identifies the root cause: a clear negative linear relationship between model entropy fluctuations and MTP acceptance rate. The fix is two-fold: probabilistic rejection sampling (not greedy) plus a novel end-to-end Total Variation loss that directly optimizes multi-step rejection sampling acceptance rate. Achieves up to 95% acceptance rate, 25% extra inference throughput, and 1.8x end-to-end RL speedup on Qwen3.5-3.7. Pre-RL MTP training with TV loss eliminates the need for costly online MTP updates. arXiv:2606.12370

Day 3 of N — Linear Algebra: Linear Transformations and Matrix Invertibility

Yesterday we defined matrices and their arithmetic. Today we connect matrices to the geometric concept they represent: linear transformations. This is the single most important conceptual leap in linear algebra.

Matrices as Linear Maps

A matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) defines a function \(T_A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\) by \(T_A(x) = Ax\). This function is linear:

\[ T_A(\alpha x + \beta y) = A(\alpha x + \beta y) = \alpha A x + \beta A y = \alpha T_A(x) + \beta T_A(y) \]

Conversely, every linear map from \(\mathbb{R}^n\) to \(\mathbb{R}^m\) can be represented by a unique \(m \times n\) matrix: the \(j\)-th column is the image of the \(j\)-th standard basis vector \(e_j\). This is the representation theorem of linear algebra.

Kernel and Image

Two fundamental subspaces characterize any linear map \(T: V \to W\):

\[ \ker(T) = \{v \in V : T(v) = 0\}, \quad \operatorname{im}(T) = \{T(v) : v \in V\} \]

The rank-nullity theorem relates their dimensions:

\[ \dim(\ker(T)) + \dim(\operatorname{im}(T)) = \dim(V) \]

Proof. Let \(\{v_1, \dots, v_k\}\) be a basis of \(\ker(T)\). Extend to a basis \(\{v_1, \dots, v_k, u_1, \dots, u_{n-k}\}\) of \(V\). Then \(\{T(u_1), \dots, T(u_{n-k})\}\) is a basis of \(\operatorname{im}(T)\). The dimension count follows immediately.

The dimension of the image is called the rank of \(T\) (or of its matrix \(A\)). A matrix is full rank if \(\operatorname{rank}(A) = \min(m, n)\).

Invertibility and Determinants (2x2)

A square matrix \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) is invertible (non-singular) if there exists \(A^{-1}\) such that \(AA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = I_n\). Equivalently: \(\ker(A) = \{0\}\), \(\operatorname{im}(A) = \mathbb{R}^n\), and \(\operatorname{rank}(A) = n\).

For a \(2 \times 2\) matrix the determinant provides a simple test:

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}, \quad \det(A) = ad - bc \]

\(A\) is invertible iff \(\det(A) \neq 0\), and

\[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix} \]

Worked Example 1: Computing Kernel and Image

Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\). Find \(\ker(A)\) and \(\operatorname{im}(A)\).

Solution. Solve \(Ax = 0\): \(x_1 + 2x_2 = 0\), \(x_2 + 3x_3 = 0\). Let \(x_3 = t\). Then \(x_2 = -3t\), \(x_1 = -2x_2 = 6t\). So \(\ker(A) = \operatorname{span}\{(6, -3, 1)^T\}\), dimension 1. The image is spanned by the columns of \(A\): \(\{(1,0)^T, (2,1)^T\}\). These are linearly independent, so \(\operatorname{rank}(A) = 2\). Check rank-nullity: \(1 + 2 = 3 = \dim(\mathbb{R}^3)\).

Worked Example 2: Geometric Interpretation of Determinant

Let \(A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\). Compute \(\det(A)\) and the area of the image of the unit square under \(T_A\).

Solution. \(\det(A) = 2\cdot 2 - 1\cdot 1 = 3\). The absolute value of the determinant equals the area scaling factor: the unit square maps to a parallelogram of area \(|\det(A)| = 3\). This generalizes: in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), \(|\det(A)|\) is the \(n\)-dimensional volume scaling factor.

Worked Example 3: Non-Invertibility

Let \(B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\). Show \(B\) is not invertible and characterize its kernel.

Solution. \(\det(B) = 1\cdot 4 - 2\cdot 2 = 0\). The second column is \(2\times\) the first, so \(\operatorname{rank}(B) = 1\). Solving \(Bx = 0\): \(x_1 + 2x_2 = 0\). \(\ker(B) = \operatorname{span}\{(-2, 1)^T\}\), dimension 1. The map collapses the entire plane onto a line through the origin.

Day 3 of N — Neural Networks: The Universal Approximation Theorem

Yesterday we derived backpropagation and identified vanishing gradients as a core failure mode of deep sigmoid networks. Today we cover the fundamental existence theorem that justifies why neural networks are worth training at all: the Universal Approximation Theorem.

Statement

Theorem (Cybenko 1989, Hornik 1991). Let \(\sigma: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous, non-constant, bounded activation function (e.g., the sigmoid \(\sigma(x) = 1/(1+e^{-x})\)). Let \(C([0,1]^n)\) denote the space of continuous functions on the unit hypercube with the supremum norm. Then for any \(f \in C([0,1]^n)\) and any \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists a single-hidden-layer feedforward network with hidden nodes \(N\) such that:

\[ \sup_{x \in [0,1]^n} \left| f(x) - \sum_{i=1}^N v_i \sigma(w_i^T x + b_i) \right| < \varepsilon \]

where \(w_i \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \(b_i, v_i \in \mathbb{R}\). The network can approximate any continuous function on a compact set to arbitrary precision, provided enough hidden units.

Proof Sketch (Cybenko's Approach)

The proof proceeds through the Hahn-Banach theorem and the Riesz representation theorem:

Step 1. Define the set of all single-hidden-layer networks as the subspace of \(C([0,1]^n)\) spanned by functions of the form \(\sigma(w^T x + b)\). Call this subspace \(S\).

Step 2. Assume for contradiction that \(S\) is not dense, i.e., \(\overline{S} \neq C([0,1]^n)\). Then there exists a non-zero bounded linear functional \(L\) on \(C([0,1]^n)\) that vanishes on \(S\) (Hahn-Banach).

Step 3. By the Riesz representation theorem, \(L(f) = \int f \, d\mu\) for some finite signed Borel measure \(\mu\). Since \(L\) vanishes on \(S\): \(\int \sigma(w^T x + b) \, d\mu(x) = 0\) for all \(w, b\).

Step 4. A lemma shows that if \(\sigma\) is non-constant and bounded, the condition \(\int \sigma(w^T x + b) d\mu(x) = 0\) for all \(w, b\) forces \(\mu = 0\) (the measure is the zero measure). This uses the fact that \(\sigma\) is discriminatory.

Step 5. Contradiction: \(\mu = 0\) implies \(L = 0\), contradicting non-zero. Therefore \(\overline{S} = C([0,1]^n)\).

What the Theorem Does Not Tell You

The UAT is an existence result, not a construction: it does not specify how many hidden units \(N\) are needed, which weights to use, or how to find them. The required \(N\) may grow exponentially with input dimension (the curse of dimensionality). The theorem also does not guarantee that gradient descent or any practical training algorithm will find the approximating network. This is the gap between expressivity and learnability.

Depth vs. Width: The Barron Bound

A refined result illuminates the depth-width trade-off. For functions with bounded spectral norm (Barron class), a 2-layer network with \(N\) hidden units achieves approximation error \(O(1/\sqrt{N})\), independent of input dimension. Deep networks can achieve exponentially better approximation for certain compositional functions:

\[ \varepsilon_{\text{deep}}(N) \sim O(N^{-2}), \quad \varepsilon_{\text{shallow}}(N) \sim O(N^{-2/d}) \]

where \(d\) is the input dimension. This exponential advantage explains the practical dominance of deep architectures.

Worked Example: Approximating sin(x) with a Sigmoid Network

We can explicitly construct a simple approximation of \(\sin(x)\) on \([0, 2\pi]\) using 4 hidden sigmoids via Taylor remainders. The key observation: sigmoids can approximate step functions arbitrarily well in the large-weight limit: \(\sigma(ax + b) \to H(x + b/a)\) as \(a \to \infty\) where \(H\) is the Heaviside step. Using weighted combinations of steps, we can construct piecewise-constant approximations of any continuous function, then refine to arbitrary precision by increasing \(N\).

Daily Topic: Military AI

3 min

The intersection of AI and military systems has entered a new phase of active deployment rather than lab experimentation. The Pentagon's Replicator initiative, launched in 2023 to field thousands of attritable autonomous systems, has delivered hundreds of drones to warfighters, with Replicator 2 now focusing on counter-UAS capabilities. The 2026 Iran war served as a field test of AI-integrated military operations, with over 13,000 AI-assisted target nominations reported by April 2026. Source

DoD Directive 3000.09 defines Lethal Autonomous Weapon Systems (LAWS) as systems that, once activated, can select and engage targets without human intervention. The Pentagon currently has more than 800 active AI projects, and DOD's 2025 National Security Strategy explicitly commits the US to dominating autonomous weapons technology. A January 2025 memo from Secretary Hegseth further loosened human-on-the-loop requirements. Source

Congressional oversight is struggling to keep pace. Senators from the Armed Services Committee have argued that Pentagon policy is not keeping pace with the operational deployment of autonomous weapons. The $13B AI budget request for 2026 — up from $1.8B in 2023 — reflects the accelerating investment. Critics point to risks of escalation dynamics, where AI-powered systems could trigger rapid-response cycles faster than human decision-makers can intervene. Source

Internationally, the UN has convened discussions on LAWS regulation through the UNODA Science and Technology Unit. However, prospects for binding guardrails have dimmed as the US, China, and Russia pursue independent development paths. China's "intelligentized warfare" vision and Russia's reported deployment of autonomous systems in Ukraine underscore the multi-polar arms race. The global autonomous weapons market reached $14.2B in 2024 and is projected to grow to $33.5B by 2030. Source

For AI/ML engineers: the technical challenge lies in building reliable perception, robust decision-making under adversarial conditions, and safe human-AI teaming architectures. Trusted AI for defense applications demands verified behavior in edge cases — a direct connection to the robustness research (GroupDRO, distributionally robust optimization) that Nishant works on.
