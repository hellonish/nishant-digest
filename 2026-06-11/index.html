⚡ PGDR Signal

Three of five papers today are directly relevant to the PGDR project's core technical approach — spurious correlations, worst-group accuracy, and provable GroupDRO guarantees.

Robust Learning of a Group DRO Neuron PGDR
Cao, Li, Karmalkar, Diakonikolas — arXiv cs.LG/cs.DS/math.OC · v2 May 2026 · arXiv:2601.18115

Studies learning a single neuron under squared loss with arbitrary label noise and group-level distributional shifts, formulated as a Group DRO problem. The authors develop a computationally efficient primal-dual algorithm that outputs a weight vector constant-factor competitive with the optimal robust neuron under the worst-case group weighting. The dual extrapolation update directly confronts the inherent nonconvexity of the loss and provides provable guarantees for robustness to both arbitrary label corruptions and group-specific shifts. Practical implementation tested on LLM pre-training benchmarks. This is a rare GroupDRO paper with provable guarantees rather than heuristic improvements.

Spurious Correlation-Aware Embedding Regularization (SCER) PGDR
Park, Kim, Lee, Yoo, Song (Yonsei) — arXiv cs.LG/cs.AI · v2 Mar 2026 · arXiv:2511.04401

Proposes a theoretically grounded regularization method that directly constrains feature representations to suppress spurious correlations. The authors prove that worst-group error is governed by the ratio of reliance on spurious vs. core directions in the classifier's embedding space, identified via differences in group-wise mean embeddings across domains and classes. By imposing theoretical constraints at the embedding level, the model is forced to focus on core features while reducing sensitivity to spurious patterns. On Waterbirds achieves ~90% worst-group accuracy (nearly doubling GroupDRO's baseline); on CelebA reaches ~94% worst-group accuracy — outperforming all prior methods while maintaining strong average accuracy. Code available at github.com/MLAI-Yonsei/SCER.

Mitigating Spurious Correlation via Distributionally Robust Learning with Hierarchical Ambiguity Sets PGDR
Jo, Kim, Chae — ICLR 2026 · arXiv:2510.02818

Identifies a critical limitation of standard Group DRO: while robust to subpopulation/group shifts, it remains vulnerable to intra-group distributional shifts that disproportionately affect minority groups with limited samples. The authors propose a hierarchical extension that constructs nested ambiguity sets covering both inter-group and intra-group uncertainties, introducing new benchmark settings simulating realistic minority group distribution shifts — an underexplored challenge where existing methods consistently fail. On standard benchmarks (Waterbirds, CelebA), hierarchical DRO matches or exceeds Group DRO. On new minority shift benchmarks where Group DRO degrades 10–20%, hierarchical DRO maintains high worst-group accuracy, demonstrating the importance of broadening the ambiguity set beyond group-level perturbations.

Reinforcement Learning Disrupts Gradient-Based Adversarial Optimization
Zou, Zhao, Aghabagherloo, Singelée, Degraeve, Preneel — arXiv v1 Jun 2026 · arXiv:2606.12251

Investigates whether training image classifiers with reinforcement learning (policy-gradient objectives and epsilon-greedy exploration) can disrupt the gradient structure exploited by adversarial attackers. Through systematic experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-100 with multiple architectures, the authors find that RL-trained classifiers significantly impair gradient-based adversarial optimization. Analysis via loss landscape visualization and gradient indicators reveals that RL acts as an implicit regularizer, producing models with highly unstable gradient directions and small gradient magnitudes that cause PGD steps to fail. Combining RL with adversarial training (RL-adv) provides a dual-layer defense: RL degrades gradient information at the gradient level while adversarial training strengthens decision boundaries at the boundary level. On CIFAR-10 with PGD-20 attacks, RL-adv improves robust accuracy by ~12–15% over standard supervised adversarial training.

TREAD: Task Robustness via Re-Labelling Vision-Action Robot Data
Kuramshin, Aslan, Neary, Berseth — arXiv cs.RO/cs.LG · v1 Jun 2026 · arXiv:2606.10918

Addresses the problem of robot policies struggling with instruction following due to limited linguistic and action diversity in robotics datasets. TREAD leverages pretrained VLMs to augment existing datasets without additional data collection through three stages: generating semantic sub-tasks from original instructions, segmenting demonstration videos conditioned on these sub-tasks, and producing diverse instructions incorporating object properties. The augmented data decomposes long demonstrations into grounded language-action pairs and adds linguistically diverse goal descriptions. On the LIBERO benchmark, TREAD-augmented policies improve success rate on unseen task variations by ~15–20% over baselines trained on original data, improving both planning generalization (via trajectory decomposition) and language-conditioned policy generalization (via linguistic diversity).

📚 Track: Linear Algebra → Spectral Decomposition & PCA

The Spectral Theorem for Real Symmetric Matrices

Yesterday we established that a diagonalizable matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ can be written as $A = PDP^{-1}$, where $D$ contains the eigenvalues and $P$ the eigenvectors. Today we consider the special case where $A$ is symmetric ($A = A^T$), which yields the strongest possible decomposition result: the Spectral Theorem.

Theorem (Spectral Theorem, Real Case). If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is symmetric, then $A$ has an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors and can be written as:

$$A = Q \Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i q_i q_i^T$$

where $Q$ is orthogonal ($Q^T Q = I$) and $\Lambda$ is diagonal with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$. The column vectors $q_i$ are orthonormal eigenvectors.

Proof sketch. The key steps are: (1) all eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix are real; (2) eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal; (3) for repeated eigenvalues, the eigenvectors span the eigenspace and can be orthogonalized via Gram-Schmidt. The induction proceeds by taking one eigenpair $(\lambda_1, q_1)$ with $\|q_1\| = 1$, extending to an orthonormal basis, and reducing to an $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ symmetric subproblem via a similarity transformation that preserves symmetry.

Worked Example: Spectral Decomposition

Let $A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. This is symmetric. The characteristic polynomial is $\det(A - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(1-\lambda) - 4 = \lambda^2 - 5\lambda = \lambda(\lambda - 5)$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = 0$. For $\lambda = 5$: $(A - 5I)v = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 \\ 2 & -4 \end{pmatrix}v = 0 \implies v_1 = (2, 1)^T$, normalized $q_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(2, 1)^T$. For $\lambda = 0$: $(A - 0I)v = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}v = 0 \implies v_2 = (1, -2)^T$, normalized $q_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1, -2)^T$. Verify $q_1 \cdot q_2 = 0$. Then:

$$A = \frac{1}{5} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\left[5 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + 0 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\right]$$

Connection to PCA (Principal Component Analysis)

Given centered data $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, the sample covariance is $S = \frac{1}{n-1} X^T X$, which is symmetric positive semidefinite. The spectral theorem gives $S = Q \Lambda Q^T$. The principal components are the eigenvectors $q_i$, and the variance along the $i$-th component is $\lambda_i / (d-1)$ (up to scaling). PCA projects data onto the top-$k$ eigenvectors, solving:

$$\min_{W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}, W^T W = I} \|X - XWW^T\|_F^2$$

The optimal $W$ is the matrix of top-$k$ eigenvectors of $X^T X$ — this follows directly from the Eckart-Young theorem, which itself is a corollary of the spectral decomposition.

Preview of Day 6: Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

The spectral theorem only applies to symmetric (or normal) matrices. The SVD generalizes the decomposition to any rectangular matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, writing $A = U \Sigma V^T$ where $U$ and $V$ are orthogonal and $\Sigma$ is diagonal with singular values $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i(A^T A)}$. This is the fundamental tool for low-rank approximation, matrix completion, and understanding the optimization landscape of deep networks through the lens of their weight matrices' singular value structure.

The Neural Tangent Kernel: Infinite-Width Limit of Gradient Descent

Yesterday we ended with a preview of the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) — the idea that infinitely wide neural networks trained by gradient descent behave like kernel methods. Today we develop this rigorously.

Setup. Consider a neural network $f(w, x) : \mathbb{R}^p \times \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ with parameters $w$, trained on dataset $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ using gradient flow (continuous-time gradient descent) with MSE loss:

$$\mathcal{L}(w) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^n (f(w, x_i) - y_i)^2$$

The gradient flow dynamics are $\dot{w} = -\nabla_w \mathcal{L}(w)$. The evolution of the network outputs on the training set is governed by:

$$\dot{f}(w, x_i) = -\sum_{j=1}^n \underbrace{\langle \nabla_w f(w, x_i), \nabla_w f(w, x_j) \rangle}_{\Theta_{ij}(w)} (f(w, x_j) - y_j)$$

The matrix $\Theta(w) \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ with entries $\Theta_{ij}(w) = \langle \nabla_w f(w, x_i), \nabla_w f(w, x_j) \rangle$ is the Neural Tangent Kernel evaluated at $w$.

The Infinite-Width Limit

In the limit as hidden width $m \to \infty$ with proper initialization (e.g., LeCun initialization: $w \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2/m)$), the NTK converges to a deterministic kernel $\Theta^*$ that remains constant during training:

$$\lim_{m \to \infty} \Theta_{ij}(w(t)) = \Theta^*_{ij} \quad \forall t \geq 0$$

This phenomenon — the lazy training or kernel regime — means the network evolves linearly in parameter space: $f(w(t), x) \approx f(w(0), x) + \nabla_w f(w(0), x) \cdot (w(t) - w(0))$. The training dynamics become:

$$\dot{f}(t) = -\Theta^* (f(t) - y)$$

which is a linear ODE with closed-form solution $f(t) = (I - e^{-\Theta^* t})y + e^{-\Theta^* t}f(0)$. As $t \to \infty$, the training error converges to zero if $\Theta^*$ is positive definite.

Key Implications and Failure Modes

1. Feature learning is suppressed. In the kernel regime, the learned representation is essentially the initial random features — the network does not learn new features, only how to combine them linearly. This is why infinitely wide networks underperform finite-width networks on tasks requiring hierarchical feature learning (e.g., ImageNet).

2. The feature learning regime. When width is finite (practical networks), the NTK changes during training, and feature learning occurs. The transition from kernel to feature learning regime is controlled by the ratio $\eta / m$ (learning rate / width) — this is the core insight of mean-field theory and µP (maximal update parameterization).

3. Connection to modern architectures. Transformers in the infinite-width limit also converge to kernel methods (the "attention kernel"). However, the key architectural innovations (attention masking, layer normalization, residual connections) are precisely what break the kernel regime and enable feature learning even at practical widths.

Preview of Day 6: Mean-Field Theory & Feature Learning

We will develop the mean-field limit of neural networks (Chizat & Bach 2018, Mei et al. 2018), where width $\to \infty$ but the learning rate scales as $\eta = \mathcal{O}(m^{-1})$, allowing the network parameters to move substantially from initialization. This framework explains when and why feature learning occurs, and provides the theoretical foundation for modern scaling rules like µP and the "lottery ticket hypothesis."
