1. Understanding Truncated Positional Encodings for Graph Neural Networks

Authors: James Flora, Mitchell Black, Weng-Keen Wong, Amir Nayyeri

arXiv: 2606.13671 (cs.LG, ICML 2026)

Spectral positional encodings (PEs) and walk-based PEs are theoretically equivalent between 1-WL and 3-WL expressive power when computed in full, but require O(n³) time and space. In practice, truncated variants (top-k eigenvectors, limited adjacency powers) are used, yet their theoretical properties were previously unknown. This paper proves that under truncation, spectral and walk-based PEs become fundamentally different: truncated spectral PEs are no longer stronger than the 1-WL test, and a family called k-harmonic distances reveals that even closely related truncated variants differ in expressivity. Key result: A mix of truncated PEs outperforms any single family on real-world datasets, and the paper provides concrete recommendations for practitioners selecting truncation strategies.

2. TRON: Tracing Rays to Orchestrate a Neural Renderer for 3D Gaussian Reconstructions

Authors: Or Perel, Hassan Abu Alhaija, Zian Wang, Jacob Munkberg, Matan Atzmon, Sanja Fidler, Masha Shugrina (NVIDIA)

arXiv: 2606.11314 (cs.CV, cs.GR)

Physically based rendering over 3D Gaussians fails at realistic relighting due to imperfect geometry, material estimates, and light transport. Neural rendering methods lack explicit scene representations, limiting interactive editing. TRON bridges both paradigms: it uses intrinsic decomposition priors from a learned inverse rendering model to regularize Gaussian material properties, repurposes a ray tracer to provide radiometric guidance (not final pixels), and feeds this structured 3D scaffold to a lightweight neural renderer. Key result: Outperforms existing Gaussian-based relighting in realism and prior neural renderers in editability and speed. Training dataset: 2.1M synthetic and real-world frames.

3. WEAVER: Better, Faster, Longer — An Effective World Model for Robotic Manipulation

Authors: Arnav Kumar Jain, Yilin Wu, Jesse Farebrother, Gokul Swamy, Andrea Bajcsy

arXiv: 2606.13672 (cs.RO)

World models promise policy evaluation, improvement, and test-time planning without costly real-world interaction, but prior work fails to simultaneously achieve fidelity, consistency, and efficiency over long horizons. WEAVER is a multi-view world model trained via flow-matching loss to predict future latents and rewards. On real robotic hardware, it demonstrates three capabilities: policy evaluation (ρ = 0.870 correlation with real-world success rate), policy improvement (+38% absolute success rate gain on top of the π₀.₅ robot foundation model), and test-time planning (+14% success rate with 5–10× speedup over prior world models). Code and models are open-source.

4. Learning to Reason by Analogy via Retrieval-Augmented Reinforcement Fine-Tuning

Authors: Zilin Xiao, Qi Ma, Chun-cheng Jason Chen, Xintao Chen, Avinash Atreya, Hanjie Chen, Vicente Ordonez

arXiv: 2606.13680 (cs.CL, cs.AI)

Standard RAG retrieval is poorly suited for complex reasoning — similar-looking problems may demand different solution strategies, while superficially different problems may share underlying reasoning patterns. RA-RFT is a post-training framework that uses gold-relevance distillation to train a retriever ranking contexts by expected reasoning benefit, then fine-tunes the policy model via reinforcement fine-tuning with retrieved analogous demonstrations. Key result: On AIME 2025 (average@32), RA-RFT improves over standard GRPO by +7.1 points for Qwen3-1.7B and +2.8 points for Qwen3-4B — an orthogonal axis of improvement independent of reward design advances.

5. EurekAgent: Environment Engineering Is All You Need for Autonomous Scientific Discovery

Authors: Amy Xin, Jiening Siow, Junjie Wang, Zijun Yao, Fanjin Zhang, Jian Song, Lei Hou, Juanzi Li

arXiv: 2606.13662 (cs.AI, cs.CL)

As LLM capabilities improve, the authors argue the bottleneck for autonomous scientific discovery is shifting from agent workflows to designing agent environments — the resources, constraints, and interfaces shaping agent behavior. EurekAgent engineers environments along four dimensions: permissions (bounded execution, isolated evaluation), artifacts (filesystem + Git collaboration), budgets (cost-aware exploration), and human-in-the-loop (easy supervision). Key result: New SOTA on multiple mathematics, kernel engineering, and ML tasks — including a new SOTA 26-circle packing solution discovered with less than $11 in total API cost.

Toward Calibrated, Fair, and Accurate Deepfake Detection

Authors: Ryan Brown, Chris Russell · arXiv: 2606.09881 (cs.LG, cs.CR, cs.CV) · June 3, 2026

Deepfake detectors exhibit large performance gaps across demographic groups. This paper introduces Face-Fairness (FF), a plug-and-play bias mitigation framework. Day 7 PCA — SVD-based derivation

Principal Component Analysis (PCA): The SVD Connection

Prerequisite: Day 6 — SVD: existence via \(A^\top A\) eigendecomposition, singular values \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}\), left/right singular vectors, Eckart-Young theorem.

1. Formal Definition

Let \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}\) be a data matrix of \(n\) samples and \(d\) features, mean-centered so each column has zero mean. PCA finds an orthogonal linear transformation \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}\) (with \(k \le d\)) such that the projected data \(T = XW\) has maximal variance in each successive direction. The columns of \(W\) are the principal components (directions), and the columns of \(T\) are the principal component scores.

Equivalently, PCA solves:

\[ W^* = \arg\max_{W^\top W = I} \operatorname{Tr}\big(W^\top X^\top X W\big) \]

2. Derivation via Covariance Eigendecomposition

The sample covariance matrix is:

\[ \Sigma = \frac{1}{n-1} X^\top X \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d} \]

The solution is the eigendecomposition of \(\Sigma\):

\[ \Sigma v_j = \lambda_j v_j, \quad \lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_d \ge 0 \]

The first principal component \(w_1\) is the eigenvector associated with \(\lambda_1\). The variance along \(w_1\) is exactly \(\lambda_1\).

3. Derivation via SVD

Let the centered data matrix undergo compact SVD: \(X = U \Sigma V^\top\). Then:

\[ X^\top X = (U \Sigma V^\top)^\top (U \Sigma V^\top) = V \Sigma^2 V^\top \]

The right singular vectors \(V\) are exactly the eigenvectors of \(X^\top X\) (principal directions), and the singular values relate as \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i (n-1)}\). The principal component scores are \(T = XV = U\Sigma\). Thus PCA = SVD on mean-centered data.

4. Proof: PCA = Truncated SVD

Theorem. Let \(X = U\Sigma V^\top\) be the full SVD. The rank-\(k\) approximation minimizing \(\|X - \hat{X}\|_F\) is the truncated SVD \(\hat{X} = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^\top\), which equals the PCA reconstruction \(X V_k V_k^\top\).

Proof. By Eckart-Young, the optimal rank-\(k\) approximation is \(U_k \Sigma_k V_k^\top\). PCA projection onto the top \(k\) components gives scores \(T_k = X V_k = U_k \Sigma_k\). Reconstructing: \(\hat{X} = T_k V_k^\top = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^\top\), establishing identity. ∎

5. Explained Variance Ratio

The fraction of variance explained by the first \(k\) components is:

\[ \text{EV}(k) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2} \]

6. Worked Example: 2D → 1D

Let \(X = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 4\end{bmatrix}\). Center by subtracting column means \((\bar{x}_1=2, \bar{x}_2=\frac{7}{3})\):

\[ X_c = \begin{bmatrix}-1 & -\frac{1}{3} \\ 0 & -\frac{4}{3} \\ 1 & \frac{5}{3}\end{bmatrix} \]

Covariance: \(\Sigma = \frac{1}{2} X_c^\top X_c = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & \frac{17}{9} \end{bmatrix}\). Eigendecomposition yields \(\lambda_1 \approx 2.370\), \(v_1 \approx [0.567, 0.824]^\top\). Explained variance: \(2.370 / (2.370 + 0.074) \approx 97.0\%\).

What Comes Next

With PCA established as the canonical dimensionality reduction method, the natural next step is Kernel PCA — extending PCA to nonlinear manifolds via the kernel trick, connecting to reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces and the Nyström approximation.

Day 7 Neural Tangent Kernel

The Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK): Infinite-Width Limit Theory

Prerequisite: Day 6 — Mean-Field Theory: Wasserstein gradient flow \(\partial_t \mu_t = -\nabla_w \cdot (\mu_t \nabla_w \Phi(w; \mu_t))\), coupling-induced feature learning, \(O(1)\) vs \(O(1/m)\) learning rates.

1. The Infinite-Width Limit

Consider a fully-connected network with \(m\) hidden neurons:

\[ f_\theta(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{m}} \sum_{i=1}^m a_i \, \sigma(w_i^\top x), \quad \theta = \{w_i, a_i\}_{i=1}^m \]

where \(w_i \in \mathbb{R}^d\), \(a_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\), \(\sigma\) is Lipschitz. The \(1/\sqrt{m}\) scaling ensures pre-activation variance remains \(O(1)\) as \(m \to \infty\). Under gradient flow with loss \(\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac12 \sum (f_\theta(x_\alpha) - y_\alpha)^2\):

\[ \partial_t f_t(x) = -\sum_{\alpha=1}^n \Theta_t(x, x_\alpha) \, (f_t(x_\alpha) - y_\alpha) \]

where \(\Theta_t(x, x') = \langle \nabla_\theta f_{\theta(t)}(x), \nabla_\theta f_{\theta(t)}(x') \rangle\) is the neural tangent kernel.

Theorem (Jacot et al., 2018). In the infinite-width limit \(m \to \infty\), with i.i.d. Gaussian init and \(1/\sqrt{m}\) scaling, \(\Theta_t\) converges in probability to a deterministic, time-independent kernel \(\Theta^*\):

\[ \Theta_t(x, x') \xrightarrow{p} \Theta^*(x, x'), \quad \forall t \ge 0. \]

Proof sketch. The NTK decomposes as:

\[ \Theta_t(x, x') = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m a_i^2 \sigma'(w_i^\top x) \sigma'(w_i^\top x') x^\top x' + \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^m \sigma(w_i^\top x) \sigma(w_i^\top x'). \]

By the law of large numbers, the empirical average converges to its expectation under the init distribution. Crucially, parameters barely move: \(\|\theta(t) - \theta(0)\| = O(1/\sqrt{m})\), so the kernel at init equals the kernel at all times. ∎

2. Kernel Regression Equivalence

With frozen \(\Theta^*\), prediction dynamics become linear:

\[ f_t(x) = \Theta^*(x, \mathcal{X})^\top (\Theta^*)^{-1} (I - e^{-\eta \Theta^* t}) \, y \]

converging as \(t \to \infty\) to the minimum-norm interpolant \(f_\infty(x) = \Theta^*(x, \mathcal{X})^\top (\Theta^*)^{-1} y\). Infinite-width networks trained by gradient descent are kernel machines with the NTK as the kernel function. The network never learns features — it finds the function in the RKHS of \(\Theta^*\) closest to the training data.

3. Lazy vs Rich Regimes (Chizat & Bach, 2020)

The laziness parameter \(\alpha = \eta / \eta_0\) with \(\eta_0 = O(1/m)\):

  • Lazy (\(\alpha \to 0\)): \(\eta = O(1)\), \(\|\theta(t) - \theta(0)\| = O(1/\sqrt{m})\), no feature learning — NTK frozen.
  • Rich (\(\alpha = O(1)\)): \(\eta = O(1/m)\), \(O(1)\) parameter movement, NTK evolves — mean-field regime.

The NTK regime is the linearized network: \(f_\theta(x) \approx f_{\theta_0}(x) + \langle \nabla_\theta f_{\theta_0}(x), \theta - \theta_0 \rangle\). The rich regime is precisely the mean-field picture from Day 6, where the coupling term \(\Phi(w; \mu_t)\) drives feature learning.

4. Spectral Decomposition and Spectral Bias

Let \(K = \Theta^* \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\). Learning diagonalizes in \(K\)'s eigenbasis:

\[ K = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i v_i v_i^\top, \quad \partial_t (v_i^\top f_t) = -\lambda_i (v_i^\top f_t - v_i^\top y). \]

Each mode decays as \(e^{-\lambda_i t}\). For ReLU networks on the \(d\)-sphere, the NTK has spectral expansion:

\[ \Theta^*(x, x') = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k P_k(x^\top x'), \quad c_k \sim k^{-d/2} \]

This power-law decay explains spectral bias: wide ReLU nets learn low-frequency functions first.

5. Failure Modes and Beyond-NTK

Where NTK breaks down: (1) No feature learning — contradicts practical deep learning. (2) Poor sample efficiency — functions requiring feature learning (XOR, parities) are exponentially sample-inefficient. (3) Width requirement — \(m\) must exceed \(\text{poly}(n)\) for the linearization to hold.

Beyond-NTK: Tensor Programs (Yang, 2020–2023). The NTK limit is the first term in an asymptotic expansion in \(1/m\). Higher-order terms capture feature learning via \(\mu\)P (maximal update parametrization), which preserves feature-learning dynamics at infinite width. Under \(\mu\)P, the NTK evolves — the kernel is no longer constant.

Connection to Mean-Field Theory: The key parameter separating the two pictures is \(\eta / \eta_0\). When \(\eta = \Theta(1)\), the NTK is stationary (lazy); when \(\eta = \Theta(1/m)\), the coupling term \(\Phi(w; \mu_t)\) drives feature learning (rich). The NTK is thus a linearized, frozen approximation of the full mean-field dynamics — exact only in the infinite-width, infinitesimal-learning-rate limit where parameters never move.
