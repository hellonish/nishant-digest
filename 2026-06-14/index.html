1. WEAVER: A Multi-View World Model for Robotic Manipulation
Arnav Kumar Jain, Yilin Wu, Jesse Farebrother, Gokul Swamy, Andrea Bajcsy
arXiv:2606.13672 — Preprint, June 2026

WEAVER (World Estimation Across Views for Embodied Reasoning) is a multi-view world model for robotic manipulation trained via flow-matching loss to predict future latents and reward values. It addresses three core desiderata for world models: fidelity, consistency, and efficiency. The model achieves a policy evaluation correlation of \(\rho = 0.870\) with real-world success rates, improves an existing \(\pi_{0.5}\) foundation model policy by 38% in real-world success rate, and delivers 5–10× speedup over prior world models for test-time planning. WEAVER also generalizes robustly to out-of-distribution scenarios, making it a practical tool for policy evaluation with limited real-world interaction.

2. LabVLA: Grounding Vision-Language-Action Models in Scientific Laboratories
Baochang Ren, Xinjie Liu, Xi Chen, Yanshuo Liu, Chenxi Li, Daqi Gao, et al.
arXiv:2606.13578 — Preprint, June 2026

LabVLA bridges AI-generated scientific protocols and physical robotic execution in laboratory settings. It introduces three components: RoboGenesis (a simulation-based data engine composing lab workflows from atomic skills), a VLA policy built on Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct with a two-stage training recipe (FAST action token pretraining + flow-matching posttraining with a DiT action expert), and LabUtopia (an evaluation benchmark for lab-specific VLA performance). The system achieves the highest average success rate under both in-distribution and out-of-distribution settings.

3. Mana: Dexterous Manipulation of Articulated Tools
Zhao-Heng Yin, Guanya Shi, Pieter Abbeel, C. Karen Liu
arXiv:2606.13677 — Preprint, June 2026

Mana reinterprets dexterous manipulation of articulated tools (scissors, pliers, shears) as an animation problem, employing a coarse-to-fine pipeline that transforms procedurally-generated grasp keyframes into manipulation trajectories through motion planning and RL. Across four articulated tools spanning different scales and joint types, Mana achieves zero-shot sim-to-real transfer for both grasping and in-hand manipulation, addressing a major gap in dexterous robotics where prior work focused overwhelmingly on rigid objects.

4. SpikeDecoder: Realizing the GPT Architecture with Spiking Neural Networks
Claas Beger, Florian Walter, Alois Knoll
arXiv:2606.12287 — Preprint, June 2026

SpikeDecoder is the first fully spiking-neural-network (SNN) implementation of a Transformer decoder block for NLP tasks. Unlike previous SNN Transformers (vision or encoder-only), SpikeDecoder is directly trainable (not converted from an ANN) and systematically analyzes the impact of replacing different ANN blocks with spike-based alternatives. The proposed architecture reduces theoretical energy consumption by 87–93% compared to the equivalent ANN baseline, leveraging the event-driven nature of SNNs where spikes fire only when needed.

5. SPARC: Reliable Spatial Annotations from Robot Demonstrations at Scale
Nils Blank, Paul Mattes, Maximilian Xiling Li, Jakub Suliga, Thomas Roth, Moritz Reuss, et al.
arXiv:2606.13497 — Preprint, June 2026

SPARC (Spatial Annotations from Robot Demonstrations with Reliability Calibration) is a risk-aware framework that automatically labels robot demonstrations with structured spatial annotations and assigns each a calibrated reliability score. Validated on 1,700 human-annotated demonstrations, SPARC significantly outperforms detection-only baselines and retains 3× more samples at high-precision operating points. Policies trained on these annotations outperform baselines in cluttered, visually ambiguous real-world environments.

📖 Math & Physics Curriculum — Spectral Clustering
3 min read
Day 9/?? Previous: Kernel PCA → Current: Spectral Clustering

Prerequisites: Kernel PCA (Day 8), basic graph theory, eigendecomposition.

K-Means and GMM assume convex clusters. When data lies on a low-dimensional manifold — concentric rings, intertwined spirals, or two moons — these methods fail. Spectral clustering embeds data into a space where Euclidean distance reflects graph connectivity rather than raw input distance.

Graph Construction

Given \(\{x_1, \dots, x_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d\), construct a weighted undirected graph \(G = (V, E, W)\). The most common choice uses the Gaussian kernel:

\[ W_{ij} = \exp\left(-\frac{\|x_i - x_j\|_2^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \]

This produces a dense similarity matrix \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\). Connection to KPCA: This weight matrix is exactly a Gram matrix \(K_{ij} = k(x_i, x_j)\) for the RBF kernel. Spectral clustering is kernel PCA on a specially normalized kernel.

The Unnormalized Graph Laplacian

Define \(D_{ii} = \sum_j W_{ij}\). The Laplacian \(L = D - W\) has the fundamental property:

\[ f^\top L f = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} W_{ij} (f_i - f_j)^2. \]

Proof. Expand \(f^\top D f = \sum_i D_{ii} f_i^2\) and \(f^\top W f = \sum_{i,j} f_i W_{ij} f_j\). Use \(D_{ii} = \sum_j W_{ij}\) and symmetrize to obtain the desired form. \(\blacksquare\)

\(L\) is positive semidefinite with eigenvalues \(0 = \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \dots \leq \lambda_n\). The constant vector \(\mathbf{1}\) is an eigenvector with eigenvalue 0. The multiplicity of eigenvalue 0 equals the number of connected components of \(G\).

Normalized Laplacians

Two normalized versions correct degree imbalance:

\[ L_{\text{sym}} = D^{-1/2} L D^{-1/2} = I - D^{-1/2} W D^{-1/2}, \qquad L_{\text{rw}} = D^{-1} L = I - D^{-1} W. \]

The matrix \(P = D^{-1} W\) is the transition matrix of a random walk where \(P_{ij} = W_{ij}/D_{ii}\) is the jump probability from \(i\) to \(j\).

Worked Example: Two Moons (4 Points)

Four points with Gaussian kernel (\(\sigma = 1\)):

\[ W = \begin{pmatrix} 1.00 & 0.80 & 0.05 & 0.02 \\ 0.80 & 1.00 & 0.03 & 0.01 \\ 0.05 & 0.03 & 1.00 & 0.75 \\ 0.02 & 0.01 & 0.75 & 1.00 \end{pmatrix}. \]

Eigenvalues: \(\lambda \approx (0, 0.04, 1.61, 1.67)\). The eigenvector \(f_2 \approx (0.52, 0.51, -0.49, -0.48)^\top\) perfectly separates clusters by sign. K-Means on \(f_2\) with \(k=2\) recovers the correct partition.

Connection to KPCA via Nyström

Spectral clustering with \(L_{\text{sym}}\) is equivalent to KPCA on the kernel \(K_{\text{spectral}} = D^{-1/2} W D^{-1/2}\), using bottom eigenvectors rather than top. The Nyström method provides the formal bridge for scaling to large datasets:

\[ \tilde{K}_{nn} \approx K_{nm} K_{mm}^{-1} K_{mn}. \]

Prerequisites: Deep Double Descent (Day 8), asymptotic statistical theory.

The Kaplan et al. (2020) Formulation

Neural scaling laws emerged from the observation that loss decreases as a power law in model parameters \(N\), dataset tokens \(D\), and total compute \(C\):

\[ L(N, D) = \left(\frac{N_c}{N}\right)^{\alpha_N} + \left(\frac{D_c}{D}\right)^{\alpha_D} + L_\infty \]

with \(\alpha_N \approx 0.076\), \(\alpha_D \approx 0.103\). Under the constraint \(C \approx 6ND\) FLOPs, Kaplan found \(N_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.73}\) and \(D_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{0.27}\), implying one should grow model size much faster than data size.

Chinchilla's Correction

Hoffman et al. (2022) re-examined with isoFLOP profiling and found \(\alpha \approx 0.34\), \(\beta \approx 0.28\) (much steeper exponents). Optimizing the symmetric loss:

\[ L(N, D) = \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta} + L_\infty \]

Substituting \(D = C / 6N\) and differentiating \(\frac{d}{dN} L(N, C/6N) = 0\) yields:

\[ N_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{\beta/(\alpha+\beta)} \approx C^{0.45}, \qquad D_{\text{opt}} \propto C^{\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)} \approx C^{0.55}. \]

This is the critical Chinchilla finding: optimal scaling spends slightly more on data than on parameters — the opposite of Kaplan's recommendation. For \(C = 10^{22}\) FLOPs: Kaplan prescribes 54B parameters; Chinchilla prescribes 13.6B parameters trained on 123B tokens.

Worked Example: Compute-Optimal Allocation

Budget: \(C = 1 \times 10^{22}\) FLOPs. Using Chinchilla coefficients \(A = 406.4, B = 410.7\):

\[ N_{\text{opt}} \approx \left(\frac{0.34 \cdot 406.4}{0.28 \cdot 410.7 \cdot 6^{0.28}}\right)^{\frac{1}{0.62}} (10^{22})^{0.45} \approx 13.6\text{B params} \] \[ D_{\text{opt}} = \frac{C}{6N_{\text{opt}}} \approx 1.23 \times 10^{11} \text{ tokens} \;(123\text{B}) \]

Connection to MoE

In Mixture-of-Experts with \(E\) experts and top-\(k\) routing, \(N_{\text{total}} = E \cdot N_{\text{expert}} + N_{\text{shared}}\) while \(N_{\text{active}} = k \cdot N_{\text{expert}} + N_{\text{shared}}\). The Chinchilla framework applies to active parameters, meaning MoE with \(k=2, E=64\) effectively decouples total parameters (memory footprint) from compute-optimal scaling, achieving a "free lunch" during training.

AI's Dual Role: Environmental Burden and Decarbonization Catalyst

The tension between AI's environmental footprint and its potential for climate action reached a new inflection point this week. A United Nations University study [UN News] published on June 4, 2026, delivered stark projections: data centers could consume 945 terawatt-hours of electricity annually by 2030 — nearly triple the combined annual electricity use of Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Nigeria. AI-related water consumption could equal the basic annual domestic needs of 1.3 billion people, and its land footprint may exceed 14,500 square kilometers.

Critically, the UNU report identifies a rebound effect: efficiency improvements alone are unlikely to offset rising demands because lower costs and improved performance drive higher usage, ultimately increasing total resource consumption. Generating a single AI image requires >1,000× the energy of simple text classification. Over 150 countries have no significant domestic AI infrastructure, while >90% of AI-specialized computing capacity is concentrated in just two countries.

On the mitigation side, the IEA estimates that AI adoption in end-use sectors could lead to 1,400 Mt of CO₂ emissions reductions in 2035 [IEA], achieved through smart grid optimization, renewable energy forecasting, and industrial efficiency. The WEF reports that AI's “climate handprint” (positive impact via helping others reduce emissions) is expanding rapidly, particularly in powering adaptation solutions [WEF].

Technical frontier: The AI in energy distribution market is projected to grow from $7.1B in 2026 to $42.7B by 2033 at a 29.2% CAGR [Persistence Market Research]. AI-driven digital twins for real-time optimization of renewable energy systems are emerging as a leading application, with models using satellite data, weather sensors, and historical trends for real-time energy output predictions. The Next-Gen AI for Energy Summit 2026 (Houston, November 2026) [AI4Energy] will serve as a key convening point for this rapidly growing intersection.

Bottom line: AI's environmental paradox — the technology that might help solve climate change is itself a growing contributor — demands governance frameworks that integrate AI infrastructure into energy, water, and land-use planning. The UNU report's call for transparency, efficiency-by-design, and lifecycle responsibility provides a framework, but implementation remains fragmented. The companies building the largest models (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind) now face mounting pressure to disclose and reduce their environmental footprints alongside their capability milestones.
