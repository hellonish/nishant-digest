Research Review — arXiv

6 min

1. Quantized Reasoning Models Think They Need to Think Longer, but They Do Not [arXiv]

Lotfi, Kirichenko, Li, and Liu reveal a striking phenomenon: aggressive post-training quantization (PTQ) simultaneously reduces accuracy while increasing chain-of-thought (CoT) length. In up to 52% of quantized-model failures, the model reaches the correct answer in intermediate reasoning steps but second-guesses itself into a wrong final output. The root cause is increased token-level KL divergence between quantized and full-precision models, which disproportionately amplifies sampling of overthinking markers (“wait,” “but,” “alternatively”). The authors propose a training-free logit penalty on these markers at inference time, reducing CoT length by 12–23% and cutting overthinking errors by up to 58% across 5 models (1.5B–32B), 3 quantization methods, and 5 benchmarks.

2. Emergence of Exploration in Policy Gradient RL via Retrying [arXiv] — ICML 2026

Nishimori et al. formalize a clean insight: exploration only has value when an agent can retry — revisit similar states and try different actions. They propose ReMax, an objective evaluating a policy by the expected maximum return over M samples, accounting for return uncertainty. Optimizing ReMax induces stochastic exploration as an emergent property without explicit exploration bonuses or entropy regularization. The algorithmic extension RePPO (ReMax PPO) generalizes the discrete retry count M to a continuous parameter m > 0, enabling fine-grained control of exploration intensity. Empirical validation on MinAtar and Craftax benchmarks confirms RePPO matches or exceeds methods relying on explicit bonuses.

3. BitsMoE: Efficient Spectral Energy-Guided Bit Allocation for MoE LLM Quantization [arXiv]

Zhao et al. address the deployment challenge of Mixture-of-Experts LLMs, where all expert weights remain in memory despite sparse per-token activation. BitsMoE decomposes each MoE layer via SVD into a shared basis (unquantized) and expert-specific spectral factors (quantized). Bit-widths are assigned via integer programming under a fixed budget. At 2-bit quantization on Qwen3-30B-A3B-Base, BitsMoE achieves 12.3x faster quantization, +27.83 percentage points improvement in average accuracy, and 1.76x faster decoding compared to GPTQ. Strong candidate for efficient MoE deployment.

4. World Models: A Comprehensive Survey [arXiv]

Zidan et al. (27 authors) provide a multi-axis taxonomy of world models along four dimensions: architecture (representation format, dynamics formulation, modality), methodological family (state-space, transformer, diffusion, physics-informed, multimodal), reasoning strategy (imagination-based planning, counterfactual reasoning, planning under uncertainty), and application domain (robotics, driving, video, RL, science, medicine, finance). The survey traces the field from early cognitive-science roots through PlaNet, the Dreamer family, MuZero, Sora, Cosmos, and Genie, highlighting the convergence of chain-of-thought reasoning with world-model imagination.

5. Ouroboros-Spatial: Closing the Data-Model Loop for Spatial Reasoning [arXiv]

Zhao et al. tackle spatial reasoning — a persistent weakness of multimodal LLMs — with a self-evolving training framework. A frozen proposer generates spatial QA pairs from 3D scene metadata, while a learnable solver is fine-tuned on accepted samples. The solver's per-sample prediction confidence serves as a difficulty signal fed back to the proposer, creating a closed loop. This uses an order of magnitude fewer training examples than static datasets. On VSI-Bench, it achieves +9.9 absolute points for Qwen3-VL-4B and +6.8 points for Qwen3-VL-8B, outperforming both open-source and proprietary baselines across six spatial reasoning benchmarks.

Math & Physics — SVD

3 min
Day 6 Spectral Theorem → SVD

Last session we proved the Spectral Theorem: for any symmetric \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\), there exists an orthogonal matrix \(Q\) and diagonal \(\Lambda\) such that \(A = Q\Lambda Q^{\mathsf{T}}\). This is a complete characterization of symmetric matrices. But what about an arbitrary \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\)? The Spectral Theorem does not apply. The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) generalizes orthogonal diagonalization to any matrix.

Theorem (SVD). Let \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) with rank \(r \le \min(m,n)\). Then there exist orthogonal matrices \(U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}\), \(V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) and a matrix \(\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) with entries \(\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge \cdots \ge \sigma_r > 0\) on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere, such that

\[ A = U \Sigma V^{\mathsf{T}}. \]

The scalars \(\sigma_i\) are the singular values. The columns \(u_i\) of \(U\) are the left singular vectors; the columns \(v_i\) of \(V\) are the right singular vectors.

Connection to the Spectral Theorem. Observe that

\[ A^{\mathsf{T}}A = V \Sigma^{\mathsf{T}} \Sigma V^{\mathsf{T}} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} \]

is symmetric positive semidefinite. By the Spectral Theorem, \(A^{\mathsf{T}}A = V \Lambda V^{\mathsf{T}}\) where \(\Lambda = \Sigma^{\mathsf{T}}\Sigma = \operatorname{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \ldots, \sigma_r^2, 0, \ldots, 0)\). Thus the right singular vectors \(v_i\) are the eigenvectors of \(A^{\mathsf{T}}A\), and the singular values satisfy \(\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}\). Similarly, \(AA^{\mathsf{T}} = U \Sigma \Sigma^{\mathsf{T}} U^{\mathsf{T}}\).

Worked Example 1. Compute the SVD of

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \]

First, \(A^{\mathsf{T}}A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 4\), \(\lambda_2 = 1\). Hence \(\sigma_1 = 2\), \(\sigma_2 = 1\). Eigenvectors: \(v_1 = (0,1)^{\mathsf{T}}\), \(v_2 = (1,0)^{\mathsf{T}}\). Then \(u_1 = \frac{1}{\sigma_1} A v_1 = \frac{1}{2} (0,2,0)^{\mathsf{T}} = (0,1,0)^{\mathsf{T}}\). Similarly \(u_2 = \frac{1}{\sigma_2} A v_2 = (1,0,0)^{\mathsf{T}}\). Extend to an orthonormal basis: \(u_3 = (0,0,1)^{\mathsf{T}}\). Thus

\[ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}^{\mathsf{T}}. \]

Worked Example 2 (Low-rank approximation). The Eckart-Young theorem states that the best rank-\(k\) approximation to \(A\) under the Frobenius norm is given by truncating the SVD: \(A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^{\mathsf{T}}\). For the matrix above with \(\sigma_1 = 2, \sigma_2 = 1\), the best rank-1 approximation is

\[ A_1 = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^{\mathsf{T}} = 2 \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{pmatrix} (0,1) = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}. \]

The squared Frobenius error is \(\|A - A_1\|_F^2 = \sigma_2^2 = 1\).

Proof sketch. Let \(v_1\) be a unit vector maximizing \(\|Av\|_2\). Set \(\sigma_1 = \|Av_1\|_2\) and \(u_1 = Av_1 / \sigma_1\). Extend \(\{v_1\}\) to an orthonormal basis of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and \(\{u_1\}\) to an orthonormal basis of \(\mathbb{R}^m\). Show that \(U_1^{\mathsf{T}} A V_1 = 0\) where \(U_1\) and \(V_1\) contain the remaining basis vectors. The result follows by induction on the rank.

ML/AI Deep Dive — Mean-Field Theory

3 min
Day 6 NTK → Mean-Field Theory

Last session we derived the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) regime: in the infinite-width limit with \(\eta = \Theta(1/m)\) scaling, the network enters a lazy training regime where weights barely move and the model behaves like a linearized approximator with kernel \(\Theta^*\). The NTK gives closed-form gradient flow dynamics, but it cannot explain feature learning.

Mean-field theory provides the complementary regime. By scaling the learning rate as \(\eta = \Theta(1)\) (independent of width) instead of \(\Theta(1/m)\), weights move a finite distance even as width \(m \to \infty\). The network learns features rather than staying in the kernel regime.

Set-up. Consider a two-layer network with \(m\) hidden neurons:

\[ f(x; \theta) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{j=1}^m a_j \sigma(w_j^{\mathsf{T}} x) \]

where \(\theta = (w_1,\ldots,w_m, a_1,\ldots,a_m)\). Under \(\eta = \Theta(1/m)\) (NTK scaling), the dynamics concentrates on the initialization. Under \(\eta = \Theta(1)\) (mean-field scaling), individual neuron trajectories deviate substantially from their starting points.

Key insight. The mean-field limit replaces the finite collection of \(m\) neurons by a distribution over parameters. Define the empirical distribution of the hidden-layer weights:

\[ \hat{\mu}_m(t) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{j=1}^m \delta_{w_j(t)}. \]

As \(m \to \infty\), \(\hat{\mu}_m(t) \to \mu_t\), where \(\mu_t\) evolves according to a nonlinear PDE on the space of probability measures:

\[ \partial_t \mu_t = - \nabla_w \cdot \left( \mu_t \, \nabla_w \Phi(w; \mu_t) \right) \]

where \(\Phi(w; \mu) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \big[ \ell'(f_\mu(x), y) \, a \, \sigma'(w^{\mathsf{T}}x) x \big]\) and \(f_\mu(x) = \mathbb{E}_{w \sim \mu}[a \, \sigma(w^{\mathsf{T}} x)]\). This is a Wasserstein gradient flow of the population loss in the space of probability measures equipped with the 2-Wasserstein metric.

Derivation sketch (energy perspective). Let the population risk be \(R(\mu) = \mathbb{E}_{x,y}[\ell(f_\mu(x), y)]\). The functional derivative \(\delta R / \delta \mu\) gives a potential. The Wasserstein gradient flow steepest-descent direction is \(-\nabla_w(\delta R / \delta \mu)\). Concretely, for each neuron, the velocity field is:

\[ \dot{w}_j(t) = - \mathbb{E}_{x,y} \left[ \ell'(f(x; \mu_t), y) \, a_j \, \sigma'(w_j^{\mathsf{T}} x) x \right] \]

which is precisely the limit of SGD on individual neurons as \(m \to \infty\). Crucially, this couples all neurons through the population prediction \(f_\mu\), which depends on the entire distribution \(\mu_t\). This coupling is what produces feature learning: each update depends on what all other neurons have learned.

Failure modes of mean-field. (1) The PDE is infinite-dimensional and nonlinear — no closed-form solution exists except in trivial cases. (2) Convergence to global minima depends on the loss landscape being sufficiently regular (e.g., convex in the space of measures). (3) Mean-field theory assumes exchangeable neurons and may not extend cleanly to architectures with strong symmetry breaking (e.g., ResNets).

Connection to practice. Modern LLMs (transformers) operate far from the NTK regime: large learning rates (e.g., \(\eta \approx 3\times 10^{-4}\) on 10B+ tokens) push weights far from initialization, implying significant feature learning. The mean-field lens suggests that the hidden representations are not random kernel features but learned task-specific structures shaped by the Wasserstein flow.
