Is Spurious Correlation Removal Always Learnable?

Yibo Zhou, Bo Li, Hai-Miao Hu, Hanzi Wang, Xiaokang Zhang, Ruifan Zhang · ICML 2026 Poster

This paper addresses a foundational question for the PGDR project: can spurious correlations always be removed by efficient algorithms, even when the invariant structure is statistically identifiable? The authors prove a conditional computational barrier: under a plausible average-case sparse-recovery hardness assumption, there exist multi-environment instances with a 1D invariant subspace that are learnable via exhaustive search but where any polynomial-time constant-accuracy algorithm would fail.

The analysis introduces a separation parameter \(\gamma\) quantifying environment diversity, showing it controls both identifiability and objective curvature. The minimax rate is derived as \(\mathbb{E}[\text{dist}(\hat{V}, V_{\text{inv}})^2] = \Theta(k(d-k)/(n|\mathcal{E}|))\), revealing a phase transition at \(n^* \propto k(d-k)/(|\mathcal{E}|\gamma^2)\) — below this threshold, estimation is fundamentally unreliable regardless of sample size.

This is the first result showing that invariant learning can be computationally intractable even when the invariant subspace is identifiable. For PGDR practitioners, it implies that for datasets with low environment diversity (small \(\gamma\)), polynomial-time worst-group optimization may be fundamentally limited regardless of sample size. The \(\gamma\) diversity diagnostic could guide practitioners in knowing when invariance methods will work.

Key result: Minimax rate: \(\Theta(k(d-k)/(n|\mathcal{E}|))\) with phase transition at \(n^* \propto k(d-k)/(|\mathcal{E}|\gamma^2)\). Below this threshold, invariant learning is fundamentally unreliable — even when the invariant subspace is identifiable.

Mitigating Spurious Correlations with Memorization-Guided Dataset De-Biasing

Arda Fazla, Abolfazl Hashemi · arXiv cs.LG, June 2026

This paper takes a data-centric approach to removing spurious correlations — one that requires no group labels and uses only 10% of training data. The authors propose a two-stage sample scoring function that disentangles the learning dynamics of core (causal) features from spurious features by analyzing memorization dynamics.

The key insight: core and spurious features are learned at different rates during training. By identifying which samples carry true causal signal vs. spurious patterns, the method selects a compact, de-biased subset of the training data. An ERM model trained on this subset outperforms state-of-the-art debiasing methods (GroupDRO variants, JTT, LfF) across standard benchmarks — with no group labels and 90% less data.

Directly relevant to the PGDR workflow. Instead of modifying optimization via gradient surgery or reweighting, this paper shows that careful data selection alone can remove spurious correlations. The 10% data efficiency is striking: it suggests many debiasing methods are needlessly complex, and that the core challenge may be identifying which training examples to use rather than how to optimize on all of them.

Key result: Outperforms GroupDRO and JTT using 10% of training data and zero group labels. Core insight: carefully selecting which samples to train on removes spurious correlations, eliminating need for complex optimization modifications.

Invariant Gradient Alignment for Robust Reasoning Distillation

Zehua Cheng, Wei Dai, Jiahao Sun · ECML PKDD 2026

LLMs suffer from shortcut learning: they perform well on in-distribution reasoning tasks but fail on inputs with different surface form but identical logical structure. Invariant Gradient Alignment (IGA) fixes this during knowledge distillation using three innovations: (1) Logical Isomer Sets grouping problems with shared logical structure across diverse semantic domains; (2) a Continuous Gradient Conflict Mask that suppresses parameter dimensions with high cross-domain gradient variance while preserving invariant directions; and (3) Truncated SVD Projection onto the LoRA manifold for parameter efficiency.

This is a gradient surgery approach — identifying and removing conflicting gradient components across domains. The mechanism is directly related to gradient surgery methods used in the PGDR project for worst-group accuracy. Results: +14.3 percentage points accuracy over ERM-SFT, and a 4x improvement in Logical Consistency Score (0.031 vs. 0.142 for ERM-SFT). Outperforms 8 baselines across 4 benchmarks.

The LoRA-efficient version makes this practical for LLM fine-tuning. The theoretical contribution includes tighter OOD generalization bounds that scale with the number of isomer domains, providing a principled understanding of when domain diversity helps.

Key result: +14.3 pp accuracy gain and 4x invariance improvement using gradient conflict masking (gradient surgery for LLMs). Directly related to PGDR gradient surgery techniques.

Prince Poudel · arXiv cs.LG, June 2026

Standard generalization bounds assume training and deployment distributions are static. This paper formalizes what happens when they aren't: distribution shifts that follow a Markov-switching (regime) process. The framework quantifies extra risk due to regime composition mismatch (ratio of calm vs. crisis states) and derives an exact decomposition separating regime mismatch from regime sensitivity.

Validated on 25 years of global equity indices, the key result is surprisingly negative: the generalization gap under regime shifts cannot be predicted from training data alone. Training-only estimators show no significant correlation with the actual gap. The feature geometry of crises is detectable, but temporal arrival is not predictable.

This formalizes a fundamental limitation for any PGDR-style approach when the nature of distribution shifts changes unpredictably over time. From Graph Cuts to Smooth Embeddings: Laplacian Eigenmaps

Prerequisites: Spectral Clustering (Day 9), graph Laplacian eigendecomposition, kernel PCA.

Spectral clustering embeds data into a low-dimensional space where Euclidean distance reflects graph connectivity, then clusters in that space. What if we skip the clustering step entirely and use the embedding itself for visualization and dimensionality reduction? That is exactly what Laplacian Eigenmaps do: they produce a low-dimensional representation \(Y \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}\) where points close on the manifold remain close in the embedding.

Formal setup. Given \(\{x_1, \dots, x_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d\), construct the graph Laplacian \(L = D - W\) where \(W_{ij} = \exp(-\|x_i - x_j\|^2 / 2\sigma^2)\). Laplacian Eigenmaps minimize a spectral objective over the embedding matrix \(Y \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}\) with rows \(y_i \in \mathbb{R}^m\):

\[ \min_{Y : Y^\top D Y = I} \sum_{i,j} W_{ij} \|y_i - y_j\|^2 = \text{tr}(Y^\top L Y). \]

The constraint \(Y^\top D Y = I\) removes the trivial zero solution and normalizes by degree. The solution uses the bottom \(m\) eigenvectors of the generalized eigenvalue problem \(L y = \lambda D y\), skipping the constant eigenvector (eigenvalue 0).

Why the bottom eigenvectors? The quadratic form \(\sum_{i,j} W_{ij} \|y_i - y_j\|^2\) penalizes placing connected points far apart. The eigenvectors with smallest non-zero eigenvalues produce embeddings that map strongly-connected points close together — exactly the preservation of local neighborhood structure we want. This is the complement to PCA, which takes top eigenvectors (maximizing variance).

Relation to Spectral Clustering. Spectral clustering with \(k\) clusters runs K-Means on the \(k\) bottom eigenvectors. Laplacian Eigenmaps use the same eigenvectors but treat them directly as the embedding. The difference is one of purpose: visualization vs. clustering. The Nyström extension (\(\tilde{K}_{nn} \approx K_{nm} K_{mm}^{-1} K_{mn}\)) provides the out-of-sample mapping for new points.

The Manifold Learning Family: Isomap, LLE, and Beyond

Laplacian Eigenmaps are one member of a broader family of nonlinear dimensionality reduction techniques that emerged in the early 2000s, collectively called manifold learning. Each addresses the same core question: how do we discover the low-dimensional structure in high-dimensional data when PCA (linear) fails?

Isomap (Tenenbaum, de Silva & Langford, 2000). Key insight: Euclidean distance in input space is a poor proxy for similarity when data lies on a curved manifold. Isomap replaces Euclidean distances with geodesic distances along the manifold, estimated by shortest paths in a neighborhood graph. It then applies classical MDS (multidimensional scaling) to these geodesic distances. Isomap finds the embedding by computing the top eigenvectors of the doubly-centered geodesic distance matrix.

LLE — Locally Linear Embedding (Roweis & Saul, 2000). Key insight: on a locally-linear manifold, each point can be reconstructed as a weighted combination of its neighbors. LLE finds weights \(W_{ij}\) that reconstruct each point from its \(k\)-nearest neighbors (minimizing \(\|x_i - \sum_j W_{ij} x_j\|^2\)), then finds the embedding \(Y\) that preserves these weights: \(\min_Y \sum_i \|y_i - \sum_j W_{ij} y_j\|^2\).

t-SNE (van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008). The most popular visualization method, though technically a probability-based embedding rather than a graph-Laplacian method. t-SNE converts pairwise similarities to joint probabilities, then minimizes the KL divergence between high-dimensional and low-dimensional distributions using a Student-t distribution in the embedding space to avoid the crowding problem.

UMAP (McInnes, Healy & Melville, 2018). Builds on Laplacian Eigenmaps and t-SNE but with a theoretical foundation in Riemannian geometry and topological data analysis. UMAP constructs a fuzzy simplicial set representation of the manifold, then optimizes the cross-entropy between that structure and a low-dimensional analog. It preserves more global structure than t-SNE and is significantly faster.

Connection back to Kernels. Each of these methods implicitly defines a kernel or similarity structure. Isomap's geodesic MDS is equivalent to kernel PCA with a specific kernel derived from shortest paths. LLE's reconstruction weights define an alternative Laplacian \(M = (I-W)^\top (I-W)\). t-SNE and UMAP optimize objectives that have no closed-form eigendecomposition, but their graph-building step (nearest neighbors with Gaussian-kernel affinities) is pure spectral clustering. From Scaling Laws to Emergent Abilities: When Performance Jumps

Prerequisites: Scaling Laws & Chinchilla (Day 9), power-law scaling curves.

Scaling laws (Kaplan et al., Chinchilla) describe continuous, smooth improvements in loss as compute, parameters, or data increase. But task-level performance often tells a different story: abilities appear to emerge suddenly at specific model scales, creating an apparent discontinuity that pure loss-based scaling laws cannot explain.

The canonical example: few-shot chain-of-thought reasoning on GSM8K. At 1021 FLOPs, performance hovers near random. At ~2 × 1022 FLOPs, it jumps from ~8% to ~55% within a narrow compute window. This pattern — negligible gains from 1020 to 1022 FLOPs, then a sudden inflection — appears across multiple benchmarks (math word problems, instruction following, multilingual translation).

The Emergent Abilities Hypothesis (Wei et al., 2022). Ganguli et al. (2022) proposed that emergence occurs when a model scales past a critical parameter threshold within a specific architectural family. Below this threshold, the model lacks the representational capacity to execute the underlying algorithm; above it, the algorithm can be instantiated. The threshold depends on both the task difficulty and the model's inductive biases.

However, Schaeffer, Miranda & Koyejo (2023) showed that much of the apparent emergence is an artifact of evaluation metric choice. When using granular (non-thresholded) metrics like Brier score instead of discrete accuracy, improvements are smooth and continous — the apparent jump is created by the thresholded evaluation metric, not by a fundamental change in model capabilities. This is the metric-thresholding hypothesis.

The Inverted Scaling Hypothesis: When Larger Models Are Worse

If emergent abilities describe cases where scaling unlocks new capabilities, the inverted scaling hypothesis (McKenzie et al., 2023) asks the opposite question: are there tasks where larger models perform worse than smaller ones?

McKenzie et al. tested 10 models spanning 6 families (GPT, LLaMA, Chinchilla, PaLM, BLOOM, XGen) on a suite of tasks including symbolic reasoning, instruction following, and irony detection. They found that for 1 out of 5 symbolic reasoning benchmarks and multiple instruction-following tasks, larger models consistently underperformed smaller ones. Example: on the "memory mismatch" symbolic task (tracking object locations through a narrative), GPT-2 Large (774M) scored ~55% while GPT-3 (175B) scored ~25%.

Why would this happen? Two proposed mechanisms: (1) Calibration-driven search space expansion — larger models explore more plausible (but wrong) answers because they are better calibrated on the training distribution, making them more confident in incorrect inferences. (2) Task misalignment — the capabilities that make models excel at complex reasoning (compositionality, long-range dependencies) may be detrimental for specific narrow tasks where a simpler heuristic is optimal.

The practical implication for PGDR. The inverted scaling phenomenon directly mirrors the bias-variance tradeoff known from classical learning theory, but at model-capability scale. For robustness, this suggests that larger models may be more susceptible to certain types of spurious correlations precisely because they are better at learning nuanced patterns — including the spurious ones. A model with 7B parameters may be worse on worst-group accuracy than a 1B model if the larger model has the capacity to memorize spurious shortcuts that the smaller model cannot.

Connecting to Deep Double Descent (Day 8). Inverted scaling is the modern analog of the "peaking phenomenon" in classical statistics (Hughes phenomenon), where increasing dimensionality first helps then hurts performance. Deep double descent showed this pattern emerges around the interpolation threshold; inverted scaling shows it can also emerge at model scale, where the interpolation threshold is re-crossed as models grow large enough to memorize entire datasets.
